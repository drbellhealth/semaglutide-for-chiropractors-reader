How It Actually Works: Your Questions Answered

These are the practical questions practice owners and patients ask most, answered plainly and without hype.

How can I offer semaglutide if I cannot prescribe it?

You offer the program, not the prescription. Your practice provides the brand, the referral, and the patient relationship, while an independent network of licensed providers does the prescribing through telemedicine. You are essentially hosting a compliant medical service rather than performing it, which is what lets a chiropractic practice participate without holding prescriptive authority.

Who actually evaluates the patient and writes the prescription?

Independent licensed medical providers, MDs or nurse practitioners, in the partner network. They review the digital intake and labs, meet the patient over telemedicine, and make their own clinical decision about whether and what to prescribe. That provider-patient relationship is separate from your chiropractic relationship, and the prescribing judgment is entirely theirs.

How does the patient actually get the medication?

Once the provider writes the prescription, it routes to a licensed 503A compounding pharmacy that fills the semaglutide or tirzepatide and ships it directly to the patient's home, usually as a multi-week supply with syringes and instructions. Your practice never stocks, handles, or ships the drug at any point in the process.

What is the real difference between semaglutide and tirzepatide?

Both are once-weekly injectables that curb appetite, but tirzepatide acts on a second hormone pathway in addition to the one semaglutide targets. In research, that has often translated to somewhat greater average weight loss, though individual results vary widely. Which one a patient gets is a clinical decision the licensed provider makes based on history, tolerance, goals, and cost.

What does brand versus compounded mean for my patients?

Brand-name products are the manufacturer-made pen injectors dispensed through retail pharmacies. Compounded versions are prepared by a 503A pharmacy for an individual patient against a specific prescription, and they are often more accessible and affordable. Both require a valid prescription; the difference is mainly in how the medication is sourced and dispensed, which the provider and pharmacy handle.

What is included in a turnkey program, and what do I provide?

The partner typically supplies the licensed provider network, the 503A pharmacy relationship, the intake and management software, and training for your team. You provide the patient flow, your branding, your pricing, and the local relationship. In short, the regulated clinical and pharmacy pieces come from the partner, and the practice-facing pieces stay with you.

How quickly can my practice realistically go live?

Because the heavy infrastructure already exists, onboarding is mostly setup and training rather than building from scratch: configuring your branded enrollment, connecting the workflow, and learning the patient-facing steps. Timelines depend on the partner and your state's requirements, so ask for a concrete onboarding plan rather than assuming a fixed number of days.

What does a typical patient visit and follow-up look like?

It starts with a digital intake and baseline labs, then a telemedicine visit with the licensed provider who decides on treatment. If prescribed, the pharmacy ships the medication, and the provider manages dose increases, side effects, and refills through scheduled follow-up visits. Your involvement is the relationship layer: enrolling them and answering everyday, non-clinical questions along the way.
