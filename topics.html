The Medications, Explained Simply

Two drug names come up constantly in these programs. Here is what they are and how they differ, without the pharmacology lecture.

Patients will ask you which one they are getting and why, so it helps to understand the basics even though a provider makes the actual choice. Both semaglutide and tirzepatide belong to a class of medications that mimic gut hormones to reduce appetite and slow digestion, which is why people eat less and lose weight. The questions below cover how these medicines move through a practice program, who supplies them, and what the science actually supports.

How a Chiropractic Practice Adds a GLP-1 Weight-Loss Program

A chiropractic practice adds a GLP-1 program by plugging in, not by building from scratch. You do not hire a medical director, lease pharmacy space, or stock refrigerated drugs. You sign on with a turnkey partner, set up your branded enrollment funnel, and learn the patient-facing workflow. From that point your day-to-day involvement is light: you introduce eligible patients to the program, hand them an intake link, and stay available for the relationship questions a long-time patient naturally brings to you. The clinical evaluation, the prescription decision, the pharmacy fulfillment, and the medical follow-ups are all handled by licensed parties inside the partner's system. Think of it as adding a service line the way a practice might add a supplement shelf or a massage room, except the regulated parts are owned by people licensed to own them. The appeal for owners is that it answers real patient demand for weight loss without forcing the practice to step outside its scope or take on clinical liability it was never meant to carry.

The Compliance & Referral Model — You Don't Prescribe

The single most important thing to understand is that you do not prescribe, and you should never describe yourself as prescribing. In nearly every state, chiropractors do not hold prescriptive authority for drugs like semaglutide or tirzepatide. The program is built around that reality, not around a workaround for it. Prescriptions are written by independent licensed providers, MDs or nurse practitioners, who establish their own provider-patient relationship through telemedicine and exercise their own medical judgment about whether to treat. You are making a referral and hosting the brand, which is a fundamentally different act from prescribing. Your job is to be accurate about who does what: when a patient asks who decides their dose, the honest answer is the licensed medical provider, not you. State rules vary on telemedicine, the corporate practice of medicine, and how programs may be marketed, so a legitimate partner structures the relationship to fit those rules and you should expect clear documentation of where each line sits.

Sourcing: 503A Pharmacies, Semaglutide & Tirzepatide

The medication comes from a 503A compounding pharmacy, and it helps to know what that means. A 503A pharmacy compounds medications for individual patients based on a specific prescription, as opposed to mass-manufacturing them. In these programs, once the licensed provider writes the script, it routes to the partner pharmacy, which prepares the semaglutide or tirzepatide and ships it directly to the patient. That is different from brand-name products such as the well-known pen injectors, which are made by their manufacturers and dispensed through retail pharmacies. Compounded versions exist because they can be more accessible and often more affordable, and they are dispensed only against a valid prescription for a named patient. Your practice never orders, stocks, handles, or ships the drug, which keeps you out of the dispensing chain entirely. When patients ask where their medication comes from, the straightforward answer is a licensed compounding pharmacy that fills the prescription their provider wrote and mails it to them.

What the Evidence Says About GLP-1s for Weight Loss

On the evidence, it is fair to tell patients that GLP-1 medications have become widely used for weight loss because clinical trials and real-world use show meaningful results for many people, alongside lifestyle changes. Semaglutide and tirzepatide both work by acting on appetite-regulating pathways, and tirzepatide adds a second hormone target, which in head-to-head research has tended to produce somewhat greater average weight loss, though individual results vary a great deal. These medicines are not magic and they are not for everyone; they work best with attention to nutrition, movement, and consistency, and they carry side effects, most commonly nausea and other digestive complaints, especially early on. The right framing for a practice is honest and measured: these are tools that help many patients, used under the supervision of a licensed provider who screens for suitability. Avoid promising a specific number of pounds; the provider and the patient set realistic expectations together based on the individual.
